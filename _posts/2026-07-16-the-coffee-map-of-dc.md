# The Coffee Map of DC (actual map below)

### Where to work, study, caffeinate, and quietly lose your mind

I am a coffee-shop animal.

During my first year of graduate school, coffee shops basically saved my life. I had too much work, not enough desire to sit alone in my apartment, and a very specific need to be surrounded by people without actually speaking to them.

So, this is my highly subjective, non-exhaustive, and always-updating map of DC spots where you can work, study, people-watch, and pretend that buying a latte counts as renting an office.

My criteria are simple: relaxed vibe, natural light, enough space, decent coffee, outlets. (in this order)

## The Potter’s House — The One That Got Away

My favorite place in DC, now sadly closed. You studied surrounded by books, the staff were lovely, and there was space, light, outlets, and genuinely perfect vibes.

I studied almost all of my first-semester econometrics on the bench by the window. I loved this place so much that after studying there once, I decided I wanted to live in the neighborhood. Very normal way to choose an apartment. I also met a lot of cool PhD people there, probably because it was the kind of coffee shop where strangers actually talked to each other. Or maybe that is just DC, where everyone either has a PhD or is currently suffering through one.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Light:** 5/5
**Workability:** 5/5
**Vibes:** 5/5

Please reopen. I am still emotionally available.

---

## Doubles — My Unofficial Office

Doubles became my headquarters during the month before my comprehensive exams. The staff kept me alive with lattes and sandwiches while I stared desperately at my notes.

Good coffee, stylish décor, lots of light, sweet staff, and a neighborhood feel: an annoyingly rare combination. The large back patio is also perfect for turning a study session into aperitivo.

**Coffee:** 5/5
**Light:** 4/5
**Workability:** 5/5
**Vibes:** 5/5

Nothing says balance like finishing an exam model and immediately ordering a beer.

---

## Reed-Cooke — Tiny but Mighty

Quiet, bright, beautifully decorated, full of outlets, and home to possibly the kindest barista in DC.

The catch? It closes at 2 p.m., which feels less like a schedule and more like discrimination against afternoon productivity.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Light:** 5/5
**Workability:** 4/5
**Vibes:** 5/5

Best for: a peaceful morning session.

---

## Tryst — Coworking, but Make It Chaotic

Tryst is the obvious destination for people who work from home but refuse to remain at home. It is essentially a coworking space with couches, pancakes, and inconsistent service.

Once, I sat for 50 minutes before anyone asked whether I wanted to order. But honestly, the chaos is part of the brand.

**Coffee:** 3/5
**Light:** 3/5
**Workability:** 4/5
**Noise:** 4/5

Go for the space and the energy, not for efficiency.

---

## Tatte, Adams Morgan — Bring a Sweater

The large communal table in the back is great when you need to spread out your laptop, notebook, chargers, snacks, and emotional baggage.

The air-conditioning, however, is feral. In summer, dress as though you are preparing to work inside a walk-in freezer.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Light:** 4/5
**Workability:** 4/5
**Temperature:** Arctic

Best for: serious writing and mild hypothermia.

---

## Pitango — Gelato Versus the Playlist

Pitango has good light, late opening hours, and gelato. These are all major strengths.

The atmosphere can be unpredictable. One afternoon, while the entire room was silently working, they started blasting disco music at 3 p.m. No transition. No warning. No reading of the room.

**Coffee:** 3/5
**Light:** 4/5
**Workability:** 3/5
**Gelato:** 5/5

A risky choice, but late hours and gelato can repair many relationships.

---

## The LINE Hotel — Main-Character Productivity

The LINE has an excellent lounge-and-jazz playlist and a lobby where you can often sit without ordering anything.

The downside is that it is extremely dark. I am basically a plant and require sunlight to function, so on a sunny day the lobby mainly inspires me to leave.

The outdoor tables, however, are elite in spring and summer.

**Coffee:** 3/5
**Light:** 1/5 inside, 5/5 outside
**Workability:** 4/5
**Playlist:** 5/5

Best for: rainy days and sophisticated laptop posing.

---

## MOOD District — A Colorful Little Gem

Bright, cheerful, and genuinely adorable. The staff are sweet, the coffee is good, and the colorful alley and back patio make it feel like a tiny neighborhood escape.

Space is limited, so finding a table is basically a minor civic achievement.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Light:** 5/5
**Workability:** 3/5
**Vibes:** 5/5

Secure a seat and do not move.

---

## Kramers — Books, Brunch, and Mild Chaos

A trendy bookstore with good food, good coffee, and a bright patio in the back.

It can get crowded and chaotic, especially on weekends, but it is a great place when you want to work somewhere lively and then accidentally buy a book.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Light:** 4/5
**Workability:** 3/5
**Distraction Risk:** High

Best for: reading, writing, and abandoning your budget.

---

## Busboys and Poets — Spreadsheet With a Cocktail

More restaurant-with-books than bookstore-with-coffee, but still a solid place to work. The food is surprisingly good, drinks are plentiful, and some locations have outdoor seating.

It gets noisy around happy hour, but that makes it ideal for transitioning from work to dinner.

**Coffee:** 3/5
**Food:** 4/5
**Workability:** 3/5
**Noise:** 4/5

My dream is to become one of the people working on a spreadsheet at the bar with a cocktail beside them. They appear to understand both Excel and life.

---

## Elle — Weekdays Only

A lovely Mount Pleasant spot with excellent baked goods and a warm atmosphere.

During the week, it can be peaceful and bright. During the weekend, finding a table is a fictional concept. Do not even try.

There is a bright room near the windows and a darker room in the back. Naturally, I choose the one where photosynthesis is possible.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Baked Goods:** 5/5
**Light:** 4/5
**Weekend Workability:** 0/5

Best for: weekday mornings and pastry-based motivation.

---

## Land of a Thousand Hills — Good Luck Finding a Table

A tiny café tucked inside Novel 14 with long opening hours, plenty of light, and a very good matcha lemonade.

The table situation is dire, but if you manage to get one, it is a pleasant place to settle in.

**Coffee:** 4/5
**Light:** 5/5
**Workability:** 3/5
**Seat Availability:** Thoughts and prayers

Best for: flexible schedules and fast reflexes.

# Bonus: Not Coffee Shops, Still Excellent

## National Gallery of Art Courtyard

The actual café situation is almost nonexistent, but the courtyard is gorgeous. The dome, fountain, and plants make even answering emails feel culturally important.

**Coffee:** 1/5
**Beauty:** 6/5
**Workability:** 3/5

Come for the architecture, not the espresso.

## MLK Memorial Library

For actual serious work, MLK Library may be the best option in the city.

It is huge, central, free, and allows you to work for ten hours without purchasing six drinks out of guilt. It also has a lovely rooftop.

**Coffee:** Bring your own
**Light:** 4/5
**Workability:** 5/5
**Budget Friendliness:** 5/5

This is where you go when the situation has escalated from “cute work session” to “this must be finished today.”

The map will keep growing—one latte, patio, and unnecessarily aggressive air conditioner at a time.
